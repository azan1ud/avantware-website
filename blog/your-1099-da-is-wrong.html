Your 1099-DA Is Wrong: How BasisGuard Catches What Your Exchange Missed

Crypto exchanges started issuing 1099-DA forms for the 2025 tax year — but they only reported your proceeds, not your cost basis. Starting in 2026, brokers must report cost basis too, but only for "covered" assets. Everything else? The IRS sees your basis as zero, and that means they think you owe taxes on 100% of your proceeds.

The 1099-DA cost basis problem

The IRS introduced Form 1099-DA to bring crypto reporting in line with traditional brokerage 1099-Bs. The rollout is phased:

  • 2025 tax year (forms you received in early 2026): Exchanges reported gross proceeds only — no cost basis at all. You were entirely responsible for tracking and reporting your own basis.
  • 2026 tax year (forms you'll receive in early 2027): Exchanges must now report cost basis, but only for "covered" assets — digital assets purchased on or after January 1, 2026 and held on the same exchange until sold. Everything else is classified as "noncovered" and the basis field is left blank.

When the IRS receives a 1099-DA with blank or zero cost basis, it treats your basis as $0. Every dollar of proceeds looks like pure profit.

Sold $10,000 of ETH that you originally bought for $8,000? If your 1099-DA shows no cost basis, the IRS sees a $10,000 gain instead of $2,000.

Why most of your assets are "noncovered"

The "covered" label only applies to a narrow set of transactions. In practice, the majority of crypto sold in 2026 will be noncovered — and here's why:

1. Anything bought before 2026

If you purchased crypto before January 1, 2026 — which most active traders did — those assets are automatically noncovered. Your exchange will report the proceeds when you sell but will not report cost basis, even if you bought and sold on the same platform.

2. Transfers between exchanges

If you bought BTC on Coinbase and then sent it to Kraken, Kraken has no idea what you originally paid. When you eventually sell on Kraken, it reports proceeds but cannot report your cost basis because the asset wasn't acquired on their platform. The 1099-DA goes out with a blank basis field marked "noncovered."

3. Wallet moves and self-custody

Moving coins to a hardware wallet and back creates the same gap. From the exchange's perspective, the deposit looks like new coins with unknown origin. Your original purchase price is lost in their records, even though you've been holding the same asset the entire time.

4. DeFi activity and chain swaps

If you've used DEXs, bridges, or staking protocols, the on-chain trail becomes fragmented. When those tokens eventually land back on a centralised exchange and get sold, the exchange has no visibility into the prior history. The result: noncovered status with zero cost basis on the 1099-DA.

The real cost of doing nothing

Many traders assume the IRS won't notice or that they can sort it out later. Here's why that's risky:

  • Automatic matching: The IRS will match your 1099-DA against your tax return. If the numbers don't line up, you'll hear from them.
  • Burden of proof is on you: You need to prove your actual cost basis. If you can't, the IRS defaults to $0.
  • Penalties compound: Underreported income triggers accuracy-related penalties of 20% on top of the additional tax owed, plus interest.

Don't assume your 1099-DA is complete

The 1099-DA is a step forward for crypto tax reporting, but the "covered" vs. "noncovered" distinction means most traders will still receive forms with missing cost basis for years to come. Any asset bought before 2026 or moved between platforms falls into the gap. That's not your exchange's fault — the rules are designed this way — but it is your problem to fix before filing.
